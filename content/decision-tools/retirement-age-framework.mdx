"What age should I retire?" is the wrong first question. Age is just one **lever** you can pull, and the plain way to choose it is to model a few and compare. This is a spoke of the [Decision tools hub](/topics/decision-tools), and it builds on the idea that [retirement is math, not a date](/topics/retirement/retirement-isnt-a-date).

## Age is one lever of four

Whether the money works depends on four levers, and age is only one:

1. **Spend less** in retirement.
2. **Save more** before it.
3. **Work longer** (more saving, fewer withdrawal years).
4. **Delay Social Security** to raise guaranteed income.

Fixate on the birthday and you ignore three of the four things you actually control.

## Run three scenarios

Instead of guessing one number, model **three ages** — early, target, and late — and compare the funding gap each leaves (the gap idea is covered in [how much is enough](/topics/retirement/how-much-is-enough)):

- **Early:** biggest gap, most reliant on spending cuts or part-time income.
- **Target:** your base case.
- **Late:** smaller gap, more compounding, higher Social Security.

Seeing the three side by side turns an anxious guess into a comparison you can act on.

## Social Security timing interacts with age

Claiming age is a lever of its own. Benefits are reduced if you claim before full retirement age and *increased* for each year you wait — up to about **8% per year** in delayed retirement credits until age 70, per the [Social Security Administration](https://www.ssa.gov/benefits/retirement/planner/delayret.html). Because that higher benefit is guaranteed and inflation-adjusted, delaying is one of the cleanest ways to blunt [sequence-of-returns risk](/topics/retirement/sequence-of-returns).

## Key takeaways

- Age is one of four levers: spend, save, work longer, delay Social Security.
- Model three ages and compare the funding gap.
- Delaying Social Security (up to ~8%/yr to age 70) raises a guaranteed floor.
