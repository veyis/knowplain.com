Saving for retirement gets all the attention; *spending* it is the part nobody explains plainly. A withdrawal plan is just a set of rules for turning a portfolio into a monthly paycheck without running out. This spoke sits under the [Decision tools hub](/topics/decision-tools).

## Step 1: a cash buffer

Hold 1–3 years of spending in cash or short-term bonds. That buffer is what lets you *avoid selling stocks in a downturn* — the core defense against [sequence-of-returns risk](/topics/retirement/sequence-of-returns). You refill it from the portfolio in good years.

## Step 2: a sensible withdrawal order

Which accounts you tap first affects your lifetime tax bill. A common plain order is:

1. **Taxable accounts** first (you've already paid tax on most of it).
2. **Tax-deferred** accounts (traditional 401(k)/IRA) next — but note **required minimum distributions** start at age 73 under SECURE 2.0 ([IRS RMD FAQs](https://www.irs.gov/retirement-plans/retirement-plan-and-ira-required-minimum-distributions-faqs)), so plan around them.
3. **Roth** last, so tax-free growth compounds as long as possible.

The order isn't rigid — blending across accounts to smooth your tax bracket each year is often smarter — but this is the plain starting point.

## Step 3: guardrails, not a rigid percentage

The [4% rule](/topics/retirement/4-percent-rule-2026) assumes you withdraw the same inflation-adjusted amount no matter what. Real retirees do better with **guardrails**: raise spending a little after strong years, trim it a little after bad ones. That flexibility is the single biggest lever on whether the money lasts — and a withdrawal simulator makes the trade-off visible.

## Key takeaways

- Spend from a 1–3 year cash buffer so you never sell stocks low.
- Rough order: taxable → tax-deferred (mind RMDs at 73) → Roth.
- Use guardrails instead of a rigid fixed withdrawal.
