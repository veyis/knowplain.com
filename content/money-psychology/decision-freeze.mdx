The most common money mistake isn't a bad choice — it's *no* choice. Paralysis in the face of a hundred funds and endless advice is a design problem, not a character flaw. This spoke sits under the [Money psychology hub](/topics/money-psychology).

## Why more options freeze us

More choice feels like freedom and behaves like a wall. In the famous Iyengar and Lepper "jam study" (2000), shoppers shown 24 jams were far *less* likely to buy than those shown 6. The same pattern shows up in retirement plans: Sethi-Iyengar, Huberman, and Jiang (2004) found 401(k) participation *fell* as the number of fund options rose. Add fear of regret — nobody wants to pick "the wrong fund" — and the safest-feeling move is to do nothing.

## The plain fix: shrink the decision

You beat paralysis by making the decision small and reversible:

- **Pick a default portfolio.** A target-date fund or a simple three-fund mix is a decision you can make once, today.
- **Set a savings rate.** Choose a percentage and automate it — see [present bias](/topics/money-psychology/present-bias).
- **Set a review date.** Give yourself permission to *not* re-decide until then. Constant re-optimizing is just paralysis in slow motion.

Good enough and *done* beats perfect and *never*.

## Key takeaways

- Choice overload and regret-fear cause do-nothing paralysis.
- More fund options can lower participation, not raise it.
- A default portfolio + a savings rate + a review date shrinks the decision.
