Every few years a new asset goes vertical, and the same feeling returns: everyone seems to be getting rich but you. That feeling — **FOMO**, the fear of missing out — is one of the most reliable ways to lose money. This spoke sits under the [Money psychology hub](/topics/money-psychology).

## FOMO is a feeling, not a strategy

FOMO hijacks the same wiring as [loss aversion](/topics/money-psychology/loss-aversion): watching others gain feels like a loss you're suffering, so you act to make the feeling stop — usually by buying something after it has already run up. Notice the tell: FOMO is loudest *after* a big rise, exactly when the risk is highest, not before.

## Herding and bubbles

Markets aren't just numbers; they're crowds. When "everyone" is piling into an asset, prices can detach from any underlying value — the classic bubble pattern from tulips to dot-coms to crypto manias. The very sensation that it's a sure thing, that you'd be foolish to sit out, is the crowd talking. Herd behavior feels safe (everyone's doing it) and is often most dangerous precisely then.

## The plain rule: cap the fun money

You don't have to swear off speculation entirely — you have to **contain** it. Decide in advance on a small "fun money" cap (say, an amount you could lose entirely without derailing anything) and never let a hot asset pull from your core, automated plan. That single rule turns FOMO from a threat to your retirement into a hobby with a budget.

## Key takeaways

- FOMO peaks *after* big rises — when risk is highest, not lowest.
- "Everyone's getting in" is the crowd, and often a bubble warning.
- Cap speculation at a small, pre-decided amount; protect the core plan.
