Money is a leading source of conflict for couples — and financial disagreements early in a relationship are among the strongest predictors of divorce (Dew, Britt, and Huston, 2012). But the fight is rarely about the spreadsheet. This spoke lives under the [Money psychology hub](/topics/money-psychology).

## It's about values, not numbers

When one partner wants to save and the other wants to spend, they're usually expressing different *values*: security versus freedom, caution versus experience. Arguing about a specific purchase treats a values conversation as an arithmetic one, which is why it goes in circles. Name the underlying goal or fear first, and the numbers get easier.

## The monthly money meeting

The most reliable fix is boring and structural: a short, recurring conversation before problems compound.

- **Keep it short and regular.** 20 minutes, once a month, on the calendar.
- **One agenda item.** Pick a single topic — this month's spending, a shared goal, one worry — instead of relitigating everything.
- **Start with shared goals.** Open with what you're both building toward before touching any product or account.

A predictable 20-minute meeting beats a once-a-year blowup, and it turns money from an ambush into a routine.

## When you're a spender–saver mismatch

Mismatches are normal, not a defect. A few plain moves help: agree on a "no-questions" personal spending amount for each partner, automate the shared goals so willpower isn't the referee (see [present bias](/topics/money-psychology/present-bias)), and shrink joint decisions to defaults you both pre-approve (see [decision freeze](/topics/money-psychology/decision-freeze)).

## Key takeaways

- Money conflict is usually a values conflict in disguise.
- A short, recurring, one-topic money meeting prevents blowups.
- For spender–saver mismatches: personal allowances + automation.
