The most overlooked obstacle to early retirement isn't money — it's **health insurance**. [Medicare](https://www.medicare.gov/) generally starts at 65, so retiring before then leaves a gap you have to bridge yourself. This spoke sits under the [Retirement hub](/topics/retirement).

## The coverage gap

If you stop working at, say, 60, you have roughly five years with no employer plan and no Medicare. Going uninsured is not a plan — a single serious illness can undo a retirement. So bridging the gap is a core part of choosing a [retirement age](/topics/decision-tools/retirement-age-framework).

## Your main options

- **ACA marketplace** — buy an individual plan at [HealthCare.gov](https://www.healthcare.gov/retirees/). Premium help is based on your **income**, not your assets, which matters enormously for early retirees (more below).
- **COBRA** — continue your employer's plan, usually for up to 18 months. Comprehensive, but you now pay the full premium. If you retire at 60, it cannot cover the whole gap.
- **A spouse's plan** — if your partner is still working, joining their coverage is frequently the simplest bridge.

## What changed for 2026: the cliff is back

This is the part most retirement advice has not caught up with.

The temporary rules that made marketplace coverage cheaper — the enhanced premium tax credits — **expired on December 31, 2025**, and were not extended. Two things changed for plan year 2026:

- The cap that limited your premium to a set share of your income is **gone**.
- The **400% subsidy cliff is back**. If your household income lands one dollar above 400% of the federal poverty level, your premium tax credit is **zero**. Not reduced. Zero.

For a single person, that line sits at roughly **$62,600**. For a 60-year-old earning $65,000 — just over it — crossing that line costs about **$10,389 a year** compared with the old rules ([KFF](https://www.kff.org/affordable-care-act/how-will-the-loss-of-enhanced-premium-tax-credits-affect-older-adults/)).

That makes the years between 60 and 65 the highest-stakes income-management years of most people's lives.

## The income that counts is not the income you think

Here is the trap. ACA help is not based on your *taxable* income. It is based on a broader number — **ACA MAGI** — and it adds back things you might assume are invisible.

**Counts toward the cliff:** traditional 401(k) and IRA withdrawals · **Roth conversions** · capital gains · pensions · **all** of your Social Security benefits, including the portion that isn't taxed · tax-exempt interest.

**Does not count:** withdrawals from a Roth account you already own.

So a Roth conversion done "to be efficient" at 61 can quietly push you over the cliff, cost five figures in lost premium help, and — separately — raise your Medicare premium two years later through IRMAA. This is why your [Roth vs Traditional](/topics/retirement/roth-vs-traditional) mix and a deliberate [withdrawal plan](/topics/decision-tools/withdrawal-plan) matter *before* 65, not after.

Model it before you convert. The [Retirement Checkup](/checkup) will show you where you stand.

## Key takeaways

- Medicare starts at 65 — retiring earlier leaves a gap you must bridge yourself.
- Bridge it with an ACA plan, COBRA (≤18 months), or a spouse's plan.
- **For 2026, one dollar over 400% of the poverty line costs you the entire subsidy** — about $62,600 for a single person.
- The income that counts is ACA MAGI, which includes every Roth conversion and *all* Social Security benefits.
