The most overlooked obstacle to early retirement isn't money — it's **health insurance**. [Medicare](https://www.medicare.gov/) generally starts at 65, so retiring before then leaves a gap you have to bridge yourself. This spoke sits under the [Retirement hub](/topics/retirement).

## The coverage gap

If you stop working at, say, 60, you have roughly five years with no employer plan and no Medicare. Going uninsured is not a plan — a single serious illness can undo a retirement. So bridging the gap is a core part of choosing a [retirement age](/topics/decision-tools/retirement-age-framework).

## Your main options

- **ACA marketplace** — buy an individual plan at [HealthCare.gov](https://www.healthcare.gov/). Premium subsidies are based on your **income**, not your assets, which matters a lot for early retirees (more below).
- **COBRA** — continue your employer's plan, usually for up to 18 months. Comprehensive but often expensive, since you now pay the full premium.
- **A spouse's plan** — if your partner is still working, joining their coverage is frequently the simplest bridge.

## The subsidy angle

Because ACA subsidies key off **taxable income**, an early retiree who lives partly from cash or Roth withdrawals can sometimes keep reported income low enough to qualify for meaningful help — one reason a [Roth vs Traditional](/topics/retirement/roth-vs-traditional) mix and a thoughtful [withdrawal plan](/topics/decision-tools/withdrawal-plan) matter before 65. This is worth modeling carefully; the interactions with taxes are real.

## Key takeaways

- Medicare starts at 65 — retiring earlier leaves a coverage gap.
- Bridge it with an ACA plan, COBRA (≤18 months), or a spouse's plan.
- ACA subsidies depend on income, so withdrawal-source planning matters.
