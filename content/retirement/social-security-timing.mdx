Deciding when to take Social Security is one of the most consequential decisions you will make in retirement. 

You can start claiming as early as 62, or you can delay all the way to age 70. The system is designed to be actuarially neutral—meaning if you live to an average life expectancy, you will receive roughly the same total amount regardless of when you claim.

## The cost of claiming early

If you claim at 62, your monthly benefit is permanently reduced by up to 30% compared to what you would receive at your Full Retirement Age (FRA, which is 67 for anyone born after 1960). 

If you live into your 80s or 90s, claiming at 62 will result in a massive loss of lifetime benefits.

## The power of delaying

Every year you delay claiming between your FRA and age 70, your benefit increases by a guaranteed 8% per year (plus inflation adjustments). This is an incredibly powerful, risk-free return that is impossible to replicate in the bond market.

## The Spousal factor

If you are married, the higher-earning spouse's claiming age determines the survivor benefit. If the higher earner claims early, they permanently handicap the benefit their widow(er) will receive for the rest of their life. For this reason, the higher earner should almost always try to delay to 70 if possible.
