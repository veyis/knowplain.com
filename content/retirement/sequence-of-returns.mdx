Averages lie in retirement. Two people can earn the exact same **average** return over 30 years and end up with wildly different outcomes — because the *order* of those returns matters once you're withdrawing. That's sequence-of-returns risk, and it's why [retirement is math, not a date](/topics/retirement/retirement-isnt-a-date). This spoke lives under the [Retirement hub](/topics/retirement).

## Same average, different endings

While you're **saving**, order barely matters — you're adding money, and a cheap market early just buys more shares. While you're **withdrawing**, order is everything. A bad first decade forces you to sell more shares at low prices to fund the same spending, and those shares are never there to recover. The portfolio can be permanently damaged even if the long-run average is fine.

## Why early losses hurt most

A 30% drop in year 2 of retirement, while you're pulling out income, is a far deeper wound than the same 30% drop in year 20. Early on, the loss compounds against your entire nest egg *and* your withdrawals; later, there's less runway left for it to matter. This is the mechanism the [4% rule](/topics/retirement/4-percent-rule-2026) is really guarding against.

## Plain mitigations

You don't beat sequence risk by predicting crashes. You blunt it structurally:

- **Cash buffer.** Hold 1–3 years of spending in cash or short bonds so you're not forced to sell stocks into a downturn.
- **Flexible spending.** Trimming withdrawals in bad years is the single most powerful lever.
- **Diversification and a bond glide.** A rising-equity or "bond tent" approach (more bonds near retirement, easing back later) can cushion the fragile first years.
- **Guaranteed income.** Delaying Social Security raises a floor that isn't exposed to markets at all.

## Key takeaways

- Order of returns matters when you withdraw, not when you save.
- A bad *first* decade does the most damage.
- Buffer, flex, and diversify instead of forecasting.
