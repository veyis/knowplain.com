A round number like "$1 million" feels concrete, but it ignores the things that actually decide whether money lasts: your spending, taxes, Social Security, and the order of market returns. "Enough" is a **spending-rate** question. This is a core spoke of the [Retirement hub](/topics/retirement).

## Start from spending, not a net-worth number

Write down what you expect to spend in a year of retirement, in today's dollars. Subtract guaranteed income — a pension, and your Social Security benefit (estimate it with the [SSA planner](https://www.ssa.gov/benefits/retirement/planner/)). What's left is the **gap** your portfolio has to cover. That gap, not a headline million, is the real target.

## Apply a withdrawal factor

To turn an annual gap into a portfolio size, divide by a planning withdrawal rate. At a 4% rate, dividing by 0.04 is the same as multiplying by **25**:

- $40,000 gap ÷ 4% = **$1,000,000**
- $60,000 gap ÷ 4% = **$1,500,000**

The 4% figure comes from William Bengen's 1994 study and the Trinity study — a historical starting point, not a guarantee. If you want a more conservative plan, use 3.5% (multiply by ~28). More on this in [the 4% rule in 2026](/topics/retirement/4-percent-rule-2026).

## Then adjust and stress-test

The first estimate is a starting line, not a finish line:

- **Taxes.** Withdrawals from a traditional 401(k)/IRA are taxable income; Roth withdrawals generally aren't. Your "gap" should be after-tax spending.
- **Sequence risk.** Shock the plan with lower returns and a bad first decade — see [sequence of returns risk](/topics/retirement/sequence-of-returns).
- **Flexibility.** A willingness to trim spending in bad years meaningfully raises the odds your money lasts.

## Key takeaways

- "Enough" = annual spending gap ÷ a planning withdrawal rate.
- 4% ≈ 25× spending; 3.5% ≈ 28× for a safer plan.
- Adjust for taxes and stress-test for bad early years.
