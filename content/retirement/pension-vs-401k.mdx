A pension and a 401(k) are both "retirement plans," but they make opposite promises. Understanding which promise you have changes how you should plan. This spoke sits under the [Retirement hub](/topics/retirement).

## Two very different promises

- A **pension** (defined benefit) promises a set monthly **income for life**. Your employer takes on the investment and longevity risk — they owe you the check no matter what markets do.
- A **401(k)** (defined contribution) promises only a **pot of money**. You get control and any growth, but you also carry the risk: bad markets, [bad sequencing](/topics/retirement/sequence-of-returns), and the chance of outliving it.

The last few decades shifted most workers from the first to the second — quietly moving a large risk from employers onto individuals.

## What a pension gives (and its risks)

A pension's guaranteed, often inflation-adjusted income is enormously valuable — it's a floor markets can't crack. Private pensions are also partially backstopped by the **Pension Benefit Guaranty Corporation** ([PBGC](https://www.pbgc.gov/)) if the plan fails, though the guarantee has limits.

## Making a 401(k) more pension-like

If you have a 401(k), you can *manufacture* some pension-like safety:

- **Delay Social Security** — it's an inflation-adjusted, government-backed lifetime income you can enlarge by waiting (see [Social Security timing](/topics/retirement/social-security-timing)).
- **Consider a simple annuity** for part of your savings to cover essential spending with guaranteed income.
- **Keep a cash buffer** so market risk doesn't force bad-timing sales.

## Key takeaways

- Pension = guaranteed income, employer holds the risk (PBGC-backstopped).
- 401(k) = a pot of money and the market/longevity risk sits with you.
- Delaying Social Security and partial annuitization add a pension-like floor.
