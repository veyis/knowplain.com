The 4% rule is the most-quoted number in retirement planning and the most-misunderstood. It's a useful **planning heuristic** — a first pass on [how much is enough](/topics/retirement/how-much-is-enough) — not a law. This spoke sits under the [Retirement hub](/topics/retirement).

## Where the 4% rule comes from

Financial planner William Bengen introduced it in a 1994 *Journal of Financial Planning* paper, testing withdrawal rates against historical US market data. The 1998 Trinity study (Cooley, Hubbard, and Walz) reached a similar conclusion: a portfolio withdrawing about 4% of its starting value, adjusted for inflation, survived most 30-year historical periods.

## What it actually assumes

The headline hides its assumptions:

- A **30-year** horizon (retire ~65, plan to ~95).
- A **diversified stock/bond** mix (roughly 50–75% stocks).
- **US historical** returns — a favorable sample by global standards.
- A rigid, inflation-adjusted withdrawal that never flexes.

Change any of those — a longer retirement, higher fees, or a rougher return sequence — and the safe number moves.

## Why 2026 is different

Three things matter as much as the headline percentage:

1. **Valuations and yields.** Some updated research (e.g., Morningstar's annual work) has argued for a starting figure closer to 3.3–4% depending on conditions and horizon.
2. **Fees.** Every 1% in fund or advisor fees comes straight out of your sustainable withdrawal.
3. **Your flexibility.** Willingness to cut spending in bad years, or earn a little part-time, does more for success odds than chasing a perfect number — because the real risk is [sequence of returns](/topics/retirement/sequence-of-returns).

## Key takeaways

- 4% is a historical heuristic from Bengen (1994) and the Trinity study — not a guarantee.
- It assumes a 30-year horizon, a balanced portfolio, and rigid withdrawals.
- Adjust down for fees and long horizons; flexibility beats precision.
