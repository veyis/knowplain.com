"Is $1 million enough?" is the internet's favorite retirement question and the wrong one. A million dollars funds wildly different lives depending on what you spend — the number that matters is *yours*, not the round one. This spoke sits under the [Retirement hub](/topics/retirement).

## The plain math on $1M

Using the 4% planning heuristic from [the 4% rule](/topics/retirement/4-percent-rule-2026), $1,000,000 supports roughly **$40,000 a year** (before taxes), adjusted for inflation over a ~30-year retirement. Add Social Security on top of that, and the household income can be considerably higher. So the real question is: does your **spending gap** — what you spend minus guaranteed income — fit inside that?

## When $1M is plenty — and when it isn't

- **Plenty if:** your mortgage is paid off, you live somewhere affordable, and Social Security covers a big chunk of your spending. $40k + Social Security is a comfortable life in much of the country.
- **Tight if:** you're in a high-cost area, retiring early (a longer horizon means a lower safe rate), carrying debt, or facing large health costs before Medicare.

The same $1M is "more than enough" and "not nearly enough" depending on those facts — which is exactly why the round number is a distraction.

## How to know your number

Skip the headline and run the plain model from [how much is enough](/topics/retirement/how-much-is-enough): estimate your annual spending, subtract Social Security, divide the gap by your planning rate. That gives *your* target — which might be well under $1M or well over it.

## Key takeaways

- $1M ≈ $40,000/year at 4%, plus Social Security — before taxes.
- Whether it's "enough" is set by your spending, location, and horizon.
- Compute your own gap instead of chasing a round number.
