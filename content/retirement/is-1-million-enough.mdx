Every article on this question gives you a withdrawal number and stops. That is the easy half, and it is the half that does not decide anything.

What decides it is what is left *after* the IRS and the health-insurance company have been paid. Nobody shows you that, so this page does.

This spoke sits under the [Retirement hub](/topics/retirement).

## What $1 million actually pays in year one

There is no single answer, and the reason is not vagueness — it is a genuine, live disagreement between two credible sources.

- **Morningstar** puts the safe starting rate at <Fact id="swr.morningstar" />. On $1,000,000 that is <Fact id="swr.morningstar.on1m" />. It is a forward-looking figure: a 30-year horizon, a 90% success target, and a 30-50% equity portfolio, given today's valuations and yields.
- **Bengen**, who invented the 4% rule, has revised his own historical worst case up to <Fact id="swr.bengen" />. On $1,000,000 that is <Fact id="swr.bengen.on1m" />. It is the rate that would have survived the single worst 30-year period in the US record, on a more diversified portfolio.

They are not the ends of a confidence interval. They are answers to two different questions, and averaging them produces an answer to neither. [The 4% rule in 2026](/topics/retirement/4-percent-rule-2026) sets out exactly how the two methods differ.

For everything below, the example household is a single 67-year-old with $1,000,000, all of it in a traditional IRA, claiming the average Social Security benefit of <Fact id="ss.averageBenefit" /> a month. It is an illustration, not a recommendation.

## What is left after tax and health care

This is the table nobody publishes.

| | Withdrawing <Fact id="swr.morningstar.on1m" /> (<Fact id="swr.morningstar" />) | Withdrawing <Fact id="swr.bengen.on1m" /> (<Fact id="swr.bengen" />) |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Portfolio withdrawal | $39,000 | $47,000 |
| Social Security (average benefit, 12 months) | ~$24,850 | ~$24,850 |
| **Cash in** | **~$63,850** | **~$71,850** |
| Federal income tax | −~$4,100 | −~$5,300 |
| Medicare Part B (premium × 12 + deductible) | −~$2,720 | −~$2,720 |
| **Left to spend** | **~$57,000** | **~$63,900** |

Roughly 11% of the money disappears before it becomes spending. That is the number that should change your plan, and it is not in anyone else's headline.

**How the tax line was computed, so you can check it.** Part of the Social Security benefit becomes taxable once combined income crosses <Fact id="ss.taxThresholdSingle" /> for a single filer — a threshold that has never been indexed and was not changed by the 2025 tax law. Against that we apply the <Fact id="tax.standardDeductionSingle" /> standard deduction plus the <Fact id="tax.seniorDeduction" /> senior deduction for being 65 or over. What remains lands in the 10% and 12% brackets.

Four caveats, all of which matter:

- **Federal only.** State income tax is on top. Some states exempt Social Security and some tax IRA withdrawals in full.
- **All pre-tax.** If part of the $1M is Roth or in a taxable brokerage account, the tax line falls sharply — possibly to near zero. Account *mix* is worth as much here as balance.
- **The senior deduction expires.** It is scheduled to end after <Fact id="tax.seniorDeductionLastYear" />. "No tax on Social Security" was never true; a deduction is not an exemption, and the benefit-taxation thresholds are untouched.
- **The Medicare line is a floor, not a budget.** It is the Part B premium (<Fact id="medicare.partBPremium" /> a month) and the Part B deductible (<Fact id="medicare.partBDeductible" />). It contains no Part D premium, no Medigap, no dental, vision or hearing, and no actual medical care.

## Is it enough? The one-line test

$1 million is enough if your **annual spending gap** — what you spend, minus Social Security and any pension — comes in under <Fact id="swr.morningstar.on1m" /> to <Fact id="swr.bengen.on1m" />, in pre-tax terms.

Spend $70,000 a year with average Social Security, and your gap is about $45,000. That fits inside Bengen's number and not inside Morningstar's. You are exactly on the seam, which means the honest answer to "is $1M enough" for that household is *it depends on which methodology is right*, and nobody knows. Work the general model on [how much is enough](/topics/retirement/how-much-is-enough), or run your own figures through the [checkup](/checkup).

## How long does $1 million last?

Strip out every forecast. Assume markets exactly keep pace with inflation and do nothing more — a zero real return. Then the arithmetic is pure division:

| Annual spending from the portfolio (today's dollars) | Years $1M lasts at zero real return |
| --- | --- |
| $30,000 | 33 |
| $40,000 | 25 |
| $50,000 | 20 |
| $60,000 | 17 |

This is the floor, and it is worth sitting with, because it shows what the whole safe-withdrawal literature is really arguing about. Morningstar's <Fact id="swr.morningstar.multiple" /> multiple *is* <Fact id="swr.morningstar.multiple" /> years at zero real return. Bengen's <Fact id="swr.bengen.multiple" /> multiple is <Fact id="swr.bengen.multiple" /> years. Everything past 30 years has to be produced by investment returns — which is precisely the part that a bad first decade takes away from you.

That is [sequence of returns risk](/topics/retirement/sequence-of-returns), and it is the real threat to a $1M retirement. Not the size of the number. The order of the returns. [Stress-test it](/tools/sequence-risk).

## Retiring before 65 changes the answer

Two things get worse at once, and they compound.

The horizon lengthens, which lowers the sustainable rate — both published figures assume 30 years, and a retirement starting at 60 is not a 30-year problem.

And health insurance becomes a line item. The 2026 national-average unsubsidized premium for a 60-year-old is about <Fact id="aca.lowestBronze" /> a year for the lowest-cost bronze plan and <Fact id="aca.benchmarkSilver" /> for the benchmark silver plan. Premium tax credits cut that, but the enhanced subsidies expired at the end of 2025 and the <Fact id="aca.cliffPercent" />-of-poverty cliff is back: for a single person it sits at <Fact id="aca.cliffSingle" /> of MAGI, and one dollar over it forfeits the *entire* credit.

Here is the part that catches $1M retirees specifically. Drawing <Fact id="swr.morningstar.on1m" /> to <Fact id="swr.bengen.on1m" /> from a portfolio, with no Social Security yet, puts your MAGI comfortably below that cliff — you probably qualify for a credit. Then you do a Roth conversion, or realise a capital gain, or take one extra distribution, and the credit vanishes in full. The withdrawal decision and the insurance decision are the same decision. Model it in the [ACA bridge tool](/tools/aca-bridge) and read [health care before Medicare](/topics/retirement/health-care-before-medicare).

There is a floor as well as a cliff. Push MAGI below 100% of the poverty line — <Fact id="fpl.onePerson" /> for one person — and you generally get no marketplace subsidy at all. It is possible to duck the cliff and land in the ditch.

## What part-time work is really worth

You will see this claim everywhere, including in an earlier version of this page: earning $15,000 part-time is "worth $375,000 in portfolio terms", because 25 × $15,000 = $375,000.

That is wrong, and it is worth being precise about why.

A portfolio funds spending indefinitely. A part-time job funds it until you stop, and you will stop. Five years of $15,000 earnings means **$75,000 of withdrawals you did not have to take** — not $375,000 of capital you no longer need. Capitalising temporary income at a perpetual multiple overstates the lever by a factor of five or more, and it is the kind of arithmetic that talks someone into retiring two years early.

The directional point survives, and it is a real one: earned income reduces the portfolio you have to draw on. But its true value is about *when*, not how much. Those first five years are exactly where sequence risk lives, and every dollar you do not sell in a down market is a dollar left to recover. That is a genuine and underrated benefit. It is just not a $375,000 one.

One trap: if you claim Social Security before your full retirement age of <Fact id="ss.fra" />, the earnings test withholds $1 of benefit for every $2 you earn above <Fact id="ss.earningsTestUnderFra" />. Only *earned* income counts — portfolio withdrawals, pensions and capital gains do not. See [part-time work in retirement](/topics/retirement/part-time-retirement).

## Key takeaways

- $1 million supports <Fact id="swr.morningstar.on1m" /> (Morningstar) or <Fact id="swr.bengen.on1m" /> (Bengen) in year one. Two methods, two questions — not a range to average.
- After federal tax and the Medicare floor, a single 67-year-old with average Social Security keeps roughly $57,000 to $63,900 of it. Roughly 11% never becomes spending.
- The test is whether your annual spending *gap* fits under those withdrawal figures. Your spending, not the round number, decides.
- At zero real return $1M lasts 25 years at $40,000 a year. Everything past that is investment return — which is what a bad first decade takes from you.
- Part-time income is a bridge, not capital. $15,000 a year for five years is $75,000 of withdrawals avoided, not $375,000 of portfolio.
