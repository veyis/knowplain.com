If you're 45 with little saved, the internet will tell you it's hopeless. It isn't — but the plain truth is that the levers narrow, so the ones you have left need to work harder. This spoke sits under the [Retirement hub](/topics/retirement).

## Is 45 too late? No — but time is now a cost

Starting at 25 lets compounding do most of the work. Starting at 45, you still have ~20 years of growth before a traditional retirement age, which is meaningful — but you can lean less on the market and more on your **savings rate** and **how long you work**. Those two levers are fully in your control; market returns are not.

## The catch-up math

Two things make the late start more workable:

- **A higher savings rate.** The gap closes fastest when you push the percentage up, not when you chase hot funds. Model it against your target in [how much is enough](/topics/retirement/how-much-is-enough).
- **Catch-up contributions.** Once you turn 50, the IRS lets you contribute extra to a 401(k) and IRA above the normal limits — and SECURE 2.0 added an even larger catch-up for ages 60–63. See the IRS on [catch-up contributions](https://www.irs.gov/retirement-plans/plan-participant-employee/retirement-topics-catch-up-contributions).

## Where to focus first

In order:

1. **Capture the full employer match** — it's an immediate return you can't beat elsewhere.
2. **Automate a high deferral** and escalate it with raises, so willpower isn't the plan.
3. **Consider working a little longer** — even two extra years adds saving, cuts withdrawal years, and can let you [delay Social Security](/topics/decision-tools/retirement-age-framework) for a higher guaranteed benefit.

## Key takeaways

- 45 is late, not too late — savings rate and years worked matter more than returns now.
- Use catch-up contributions from 50 (and the larger 60–63 catch-up).
- Match first, automate, and consider a slightly later date.
