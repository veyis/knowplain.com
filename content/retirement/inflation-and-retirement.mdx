A market crash is loud; inflation is silent. It doesn't show up as a bad day — it slowly erodes what your income buys over decades. For a 30-year retirement, that's the risk most plans underweight. This spoke sits under the [Retirement hub](/topics/retirement).

## Why inflation hits retirees harder

While you're working, wages tend to rise with prices. In retirement, much of your income is fixed in dollar terms, so rising prices come straight out of your standard of living. This is why the rigid, inflation-adjusted withdrawal behind [the 4% rule](/topics/retirement/4-percent-rule-2026) can strain a portfolio — the withdrawals grow every year even when markets don't. A $40,000 withdrawal held flat for 30 years isn't a stable plan; it's a slow pay cut.

## What it actually costs over time

> **Rule of 72:** divide 72 by the inflation rate to estimate how many years it takes prices to double. At 3%, that's about 24 years — well within a normal retirement.

At just 2.5% inflation, a $40,000 lifestyle costs steadily more to maintain:

- **After 10 years:** ~$51,200
- **After 20 years:** ~$65,500
- **After 30 years:** ~$83,900

By the end of a long retirement you may need to withdraw **more than double** your starting amount just to buy the same groceries — which is exactly why a "4%" withdrawal rate isn't really 4% in year 30.

## Sequence-of-inflation risk

We usually talk about [sequence-of-returns risk](/topics/retirement/sequence-of-returns) — a market crash early in retirement. There's a mirror image: **sequence-of-inflation risk**. A price spike early on (as in the 1970s or the early 2020s) permanently ratchets up your withdrawals from the start, draining the portfolio faster than a plan built on average inflation assumes.

## Plain defenses

You can't stop inflation, but you can blunt it:

- **Keep some growth assets.** Stocks have historically outpaced inflation over long horizons better than cash.
- **Lean on inflation-linked income.** Social Security includes an annual cost-of-living adjustment ([SSA COLA](https://www.ssa.gov/cola/)), and [Treasury Inflation-Protected Securities](https://www.treasurydirect.gov/marketable-securities/tips/) adjust with prices — one reason [delaying Social Security](/topics/retirement/social-security-timing) is valuable.
- **Stay flexible.** Trimming spending in high-inflation years protects the portfolio the same way it protects against sequence risk.

## Key takeaways

- Inflation is a slow, compounding risk over a 30-year retirement.
- At 2.5%, a $40k lifestyle costs ~$83,900 after 30 years — more than double.
- Defend with growth assets, COLA/TIPS income, and spending flexibility.
