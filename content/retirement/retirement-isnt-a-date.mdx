Most people pick a birthday and call it a plan. The plainer view — the idea behind everything in the [Retirement hub](/topics/retirement) — is that retirement is a **funding problem**. The date is what falls out of the math, not the thing you choose first.

## Why the date is the wrong input

A date answers "when do I want to stop?" It says nothing about whether the money lasts. Two people who retire at the same age can have completely different outcomes depending on how much they spend, how much they saved, and what markets do in their first decade. Start from the date and you're guessing. Start from the math and the date reveals itself.

## The plain model: spending → rate → portfolio

Reverse the problem into three steps:

1. **Spending.** Write down annual spending in today's dollars — the number you actually live on, minus any pension or Social Security you'll receive. The Social Security Administration's [retirement planner](https://www.ssa.gov/benefits/retirement/planner/) estimates your benefit.
2. **Withdrawal rate.** Pick a planning rate. A common starting heuristic is 4% a year, from William Bengen's 1994 research and the later Trinity study — useful as a first pass, not a promise. See [the 4% rule in 2026](/topics/retirement/4-percent-rule-2026).
3. **Portfolio.** Divide the spending gap by the rate. At 4%, that's the same as multiplying spending by 25. Details in [how much is enough](/topics/retirement/how-much-is-enough).

Now the "right age" is simply the year your projected portfolio meets that target — and the gap tells you which lever to pull.

## Then stress-test it

A single average return hides the risk that matters most: **sequence of returns**. A bad decade early in retirement, while you're withdrawing, does far more damage than the same decade later. Build in a cash buffer and flexible spending rather than trying to predict crashes — see [sequence of returns risk](/topics/retirement/sequence-of-returns).

## Key takeaways

- The date is an **output** of the math, not an input.
- Model spending first, then back into the portfolio you need.
- 4% / 25× is a planning heuristic — stress-test it for bad early years.
