"Roth or Traditional?" sounds like a tax-code puzzle. Plainly, it's one question with two answers, and the whole thing turns on *when* you'd rather pay tax. This spoke sits under the [Retirement hub](/topics/retirement).

## The core trade

- **Traditional** (401(k)/IRA): you deduct the contribution now and pay income tax on withdrawals in retirement. Tax break *today*.
- **Roth**: you contribute after-tax dollars now and withdrawals in retirement are generally tax-free. Tax break *later*. See the IRS [Roth comparison chart](https://www.irs.gov/retirement-plans/roth-comparison-chart).

Same money, two different timing bets on your own tax rate.

## When each one wins

- **Roth wins** if you expect a **higher** tax rate in retirement than today — common for younger savers, people early in their careers, or anyone expecting tax rates to rise.
- **Traditional wins** if you expect a **lower** rate later — common for high earners in their peak years who'll drop to a smaller bracket in retirement.

Since you're guessing about future tax law and your own future income, the honest answer is "nobody knows for sure."

## The 2026 wrinkle: it is not only about tax rates

Most explanations stop at "compare your tax rate now and later." That is incomplete, and the gap is expensive.

Traditional withdrawals and **Roth conversions** raise your reported income. Roth withdrawals do not. Two things key off that income figure:

- **Before 65 — ACA subsidies.** For 2026, income one dollar over 400% of the federal poverty line wipes out your entire premium tax credit. A conversion done "to be efficient" at 61 can cost five figures. See [health care before Medicare](/topics/retirement/health-care-before-medicare).
- **After 63 — Medicare IRMAA.** Medicare surcharges are based on your income from **two years earlier**, so a conversion at 63 shows up on your Part B premium at 65.

None of this means avoid conversions. It means the years between 60 and 65 are when the Roth question stops being theoretical and starts having a dollar value attached. Model it before you convert.

## One more rule if you are a high earner

From 2026, if your prior-year Social Security wages from your plan's employer exceeded **$150,000**, SECURE 2.0 requires your **catch-up contributions to be Roth** — the pre-tax option is no longer available to you for that portion.

## The plain default: hedge

Because the future is unknowable, many people **split** — some Traditional, some Roth — to hedge their tax bet and gain flexibility in retirement. Having both lets you manage your taxable income year by year (see [the withdrawal plan](/topics/decision-tools/withdrawal-plan)). And regardless of type: **capture the full employer match first** — that beats the Roth-vs-Traditional question entirely.

## Key takeaways

- Traditional = tax break now; Roth = tax-free later.
- Roth if your future rate is higher; Traditional if it's lower.
- When unsure, split the difference — and always grab the match first.
